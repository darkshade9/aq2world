Response to Game Critics Slam Violent Mods

Editorial - Tristan 'Jherax' Blease


After reading through the article that was originally posted in the Wall Street Journal about minor mod makers being the moral equivelent of teenage pornographers, I was utterly offended. I took the time to mail the National Institute on Media and the Family. First, here is a copy of the mail I sent off to them, followed by their response.



My Letter:

David Walsh,

I am writing in response to a comment associated with you on an article recently put online at ZDNet's website

David Walsh, president of National Institute on Media and the Family, a nonpartisan organization that looks at the impact of entertainment on children, contends that underage mod makers are the moral equivalent of teenage pornographers. He thinks the game industry should apply the same rating system used on games to the mods themselves and restrict kids' access to Web sites where mods are posted.

As a minor whom has worked on numerous modification projects and currently runs a large editing web site, I am extremely offended by that statement.

First of all, I do realize that many mods are extremely violent, however -- the morals behind violence do not have anything to do with the morals behind pornography in any sort of fashion. Most mod authors (including minors) do it because they enjoy game editing and are artists or coders. Secondly, you cannot assume that every single mod author whom is under-aged is a victim of faulty morals. Many of the mod authors I know under the age of 18 share the same morals as any other kid you find on the street. I myself am active in the Boy Scouts of America and will continue to be active in the years to come.

To assualt minor mod makers in this fashion IMHO is ignorant, as their are other industries that have a much greater negative impact on youth (the music industry comes to mind).



Their Response:

Dr. David Walsh and the National Institute on Media and the family were cited in an article about violent software modifications for video games ("mods") in Tuesday, August 28, 2001's Wall Street Journal. While the article accurately portrayed our position on mods for the most part, we do feel that the article misrepresents our stance on a key point.

The erroneous sentence reads: "David Walsh, president of the National Institute on Media and the Family, a nonpartisan organization that looks at the impact of entertainment on children, contends that underage mod makers are the moral equivalent of teenage pornographers."

It is not the opinion of Dr. Walsh nor is it the official position of the National Institute on Media and the Family that violent software mods and teenage pornography are morally "equivalent." To equate these two phenomena is to engage in reckless exaggeration that confuses and sabotages two very important issues for today's children and families. Software mods and teenage pornography are both potentially harmful to children, but to different degrees and in different ways.

It is our understanding that this misrepresentation was due to confusion over a question by reporter Khan T. L. Tran to Dr. Walsh about similarities between software mods and other child-related media issues. We believe the absence of any quotation on the matter is evidence that Dr. Walsh neither stated nor implied that the two issue are equivalent.

As the article correctly reported, it is the position of the National Institute on Media and the Family that "the game industry should apply the same ratings system used on games to the mods themselves and restrict kids' access to Web sites where mods are posted."

The National Institute on Media and the Family regrets any confusion due to this error.
