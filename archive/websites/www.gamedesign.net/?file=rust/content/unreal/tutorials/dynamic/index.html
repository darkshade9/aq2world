Dynamic Lighting on Movers Technique Originally by Joel Caesar


One of the biggest complaints people have/had about Quake2, is the lighting on entities. A door, for example, is rendered with lighting based on it's position (open or closed) at compile time. The same problem exists for any type of Quake entity.

With Unreal, those days are gone. This is probably one of the best new features in any first-person shooter. It also helps Unreal jump to the top of the "Best Looking Games" list. The following tutorial assumes you know how to build a mover brush, and set its keyframes.

Creating the Mover and Setting Properties

First, build a room, and put in a lift (created from a mover brush). Put a light above the lift, and just off to the side, near the top of the room. The room should be large enough (and the radius of the light small enough) that only the top half of the room is lit. If you were to compile your map now, you'd probably find that the lift will retain it's lighting as it was rendered while it was down, away from the light. If you had the lift start in the up position, you'd find the lift rendered with lighting on it all the time. Neither of these situations is good at all.

Bring up the properties on the mover brush (your lift), and go to the key: Mover->bDynamicLightM...

I can't tell you the entire key, as the window isn't resizeable (I think it is bDynamicLightMover). It is the second key down under Mover (right below bDamageTrigg...). Set it to true, recompile your map, and then run it in Unreal.

Now as your lift moves closer to the light, it gets brighter gradually (as it should). As it moves away from the light, it naturally gets darker.

Dynamic Lighting Problems

Great you say, but any problems? Yes. Depending on where the pivot point for the mover is, your mover may be rendered unlit (basically black, even with a flare, searchlight, or flashlight, on it).

This happens, because the pivot point of the mover is starting somewhere outside your level (or in a position too far away from your source light--details to follow). I've found a way to "fix" this, and while it seems simple enough, so far it requires rebuilding your mover from scratch

In the first figure, I've built (and subtracted) a building room off to the side of my level. In it, I built the mover (a lift in this case), which is shown in blue. I've highlighted the building room's walls, and selected the Copy Polygon -> To Brush option (right click). The red cross in the middle, is the center of the building room brush. The most important thing about the pivot point is to make sure that it will be in a part of your finished mover that doesn't go into the void (outside your level). Since the platform (top, flat part) of my lift will always be in the level, I moved the mover brushes so the pivot point will be near the top of the lift. Now all your need to do is set your textures (you can't change mover textures later), recompile, and then intersect. Now you have your custom brush to build mover brushes with.

In this figure, you can see I've moved my new custom, or "template" building brush into place in a room in my level (which for now is just a big subtracted box). Select the AddMover button, and set its properties as detailed in the beginning of this article (also set your keyframes).

Ideally, you want the pivot point to start inside your level (as shown by pivot point in the figure). As the mover (or in my case, lift) moves up, the pivot point gets closer to the light (shown in green, upper left corner), and the mover gets brighter. When the lift is up, the pivot point should be at point A. Unreal light calculations are determined by the position of the pivot point

The unlit mover lighting problem happens when you build your mover centered in the building box. This results in a pivot point which would be at point C in your "template" brush. This is clearly outside of the level (the area below the horizontal line at point B). When Unreal dynamically calculates light for the mover, the mover gets NO light calculated for it, because the pivot point is outside the level. As the mover rises, it stays unlit until it reaches point B. From this point light is calculated, and the mover flashes from black, to whatever the light level is at point B. Dynamic lighting continues to gradually brighten until the lift stops (point B to point A)

By making sure your pivot points for movers stay within the level, you won't get those undesireable lighting flashes. Also, your mover shouldn't be rendered unlit in the dark areas anymore.

Another problem associated with dynamic lighting, will be the speed of the game. Check your FPS and polygon counts with the "stats fps" command in the console--just like any cool feature, too much is too much.
