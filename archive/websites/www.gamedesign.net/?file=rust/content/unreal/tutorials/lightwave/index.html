Importing Brushes into UED with Lightwave - Fishman


One of the most powerful abilities of Unreal editing is importing brushes from commercial 3d programs. This allows designers to create amazingly detailed architecture that would be impossible with other game editors.

What's Lightwave?

First of all it might help to have lightwave 5.5. The price of is currently $780 so if you don't have it you might be out of luck unfortunately, unless your a rich p1mp or something...like me. =)

ok, I have it, what now?

Open up Lightwave Modeler. Since unreal's polygon engine is based on triangles your going to have to set Lightwave up to use triangles. Goto options under the objects menu and click on triangles. Click ok.

time to create the stuff

Now its time to create your brush or group of brushes your going to want to import into unreal. By default the grid in lightwave is in mm. Considering that one single unit in UED is equal to 1 meter, we are going to have to change the grid of lightwave, or you will have to scale and resize a lot in UED. To change the grid in lightwave. Goto Display, Options, and set the grid to 1 m instead of 1 mm. Click ok.

Now create your brush. It's probably a good idea to keep your polygon count below 500, or Unreal will freak out. To check the # of polygons...goto display, then statistics, and that first number will be the # of polygons.

Here is the shot of the rock that I made. hehe...not a very good rock, but...oh well..you get the point.

After you've made what you want to import into UED you need to export it from Lightwave.

Goto tools, custom, and then LW_Translator3D-export. Click on DXF, and save the file WITH the extension DXF, lightwave doesnt seem to do the extension for you.

importing into UED

Now open UnrealED, open the map you want to add the new brush to goto brush, import, and find the file you just saved. Then click ok after the import window pops up. For now don't keep everything at default.

Here is a shot of my rock in UnrealED(after being scaled and rotated to create a more random feel)

the first thing you will probably want to do is to resize it to what you want. Remember though that if you scale the brush you are going to have to reset the texture scale of the brush to 1 after you add it to the world. Now you can even duplicate this one rock brush and create many different rocks by using the scale, rotate, and resize tools.

here is a shot of 3 copies of the rocks each altered with rotate, scale, shear, and resize.

I assume if your reading this you know how to scale brushes and textures, add brushes to the world,etc.

Problems?

Joel Caesar sent along this important bit of info on a problem with imported brushes.

Unreal's bCastShadows (or is it bShadowCast) is not yet implemented, so your brush cannot be set to cast shadows (like on the ground). The brush will be lighted correctly however. So it is important to place the brush in an area either dark enough, or light enough that you don't need to see it casting a shadow.

Another tip i received from Haze about some potential problems and solutions.

1 METER (not mm) in Lightwave = 1 unit in UnrealEd. This means making your Lightwave models BIG. the default sphere, for example, has a diameter of 1 meter, hence the size of only one unit in UnrealEd.

Lightwaves vertex ordering is reversed from most other 3D programs, this means that models will often import with their polygons facing inwards, this doesn't always happen with UnrealEd, but will sometimes. If it does then you can flip the polygons in Lightwave before exporting.

On a personal note... Importing geometry from other programs is a great way of creating detail, but the fact that UnrealEd imports everything as triangles (you can use polygons with sides>3 in Lightwave, the DXF export or UnrealEd converts them to triangles) does tend to clutter up the wire frame display. When I've looked through the Games maps I've not seen many brushes with triangular faces, the ones that I have seen look like they were designed in the 2D Shaper, rather than importing.

Conclusion?

Creating brushes in 3d programs like lightwave is a very powerful ability of Unreal editing and allows for some amazing things that were previously impossible. Although there are a few problems, I think that it is an amazing feature. uummm...ok...that was a cheesy conclusion.

I'm far from an expert with Lightwave and i might of made some errors. Also if anyone knows how to make the Lightwave stuff import with the correct scale and size then please email a Rust admin. Thanks.
