Community Profile



The Community Profile section is a biweekly section that profiles important people in the gamedesign/level design community.

David Hyde


David Hyde has spent a lot of time answering questions in the Quake 2 and other discussion boards here at Rust.  Additionally, he has written tutorials to help mappers better understand the Quake 2 engine.  David also has created two excellent applications to make life easier for the Quake 2 (and soon the Half-Life mapper).  These programs are MapSpy, an error troubleshooting tool and GenSurf, a tool to make it easier to create realistic outdoor areas.

David has agreed to answer a few questions for us, that you can read below.

For those out there that don't know, tell us a little about yourself.

I'm a structural engineer by education but since '85 or so have been parked in front of a PC writing software. Most of the products I've produced have been related to weapons effects (except one... now back to our program...), obviously targeting a very limited audience. It's a great job, but the restrictions get to be a bit tedious at times. That's one of the fun things about writing game-related software - I feel like the handcuffs have been taken off :-) I have a beautiful wife and three great kids, ages 7, 11, and 15. Life is good.


What inspired you to write the tutorials you've done?

Same as you - so we can bitch at new guys who don't read them! Not really. I don't know about you, but I've written tutorials for mostly selfish reasons - the same reasons I've tried to answer as many questions on the discussion board as I can. You can learn a lot by trying to teach others. Now if I could just take that darn SP map back and do it right this time....


What inspired you to create GenSurf and MapSpy?

MapSpy came about from a mutual frustration with "Index overflow" errors that I shared with Mad Dog. I had already released my only map after stripping out large portions of it, reducing the number and size of textures used, and sacrificing a goat, *none* of which had anything to do with the error. When MD started experiencing the same problems in his own map, we teamed up and went through a couple of weeks of picking the skulls of industry gurus trying to figure out exactly what was causing the error. We'd have never achieved the results we did without the help of Peter Mack at Rogue. I'm still amazed at the patience of that guy - he endured 2-3 e-mails a day from me for a while, and was always very gracious (and more importantly, always correct) with his answers. I have a lot of respect for the intelligence of John Carmack, but contrast Peter Mack's helpfulness with Carmack's response to the problem - I swear this is a quote: "Q3 Arena will not have this limitation." Thank you so much, John! Heh.

The first version of MapSpy did nothing but warn if your map would cause an Index overflow error. Since that time Mad Dog and I have been a good team, in my (not so) humble opinion. We're both pretty obsessive about this stuff, and our different styles compliment each other, I think. I'm a "use a bigger hammer" kind of guy, while he has the intellect to think things through. And the rest is history... :-)

GenSurf has existed in bits and pieces for far longer than Quake2 has been around, and I've fooled around with writing code to produce different sorts of map objects since the game came out. I just never thought of any of this stuff as being particularly useful to others. I suppose that attitude is largely due to my being a programmer. When I first learned of ArghRad 2.0's Phong shading I immediately had an application in mind - this would look *great* in an outdoor area. So when it became available I had an example map ready to go to show off screenshots with. My good pal Monsto asked whether the surfaces in the screenshots were generated with mgs, and when I replied that I used my own stuff I received several requests for copies of the code.

At that time, though, the user interface was non-existent - I just hard-wired values in and re-compiled when I wanted a different surface. I got enough requests for the code that I thought, well, maybe it would be useful after all. Coincidentally I happened to be bugging Alexander Malmberg at about the same time on a different subject, and he provided the tip that makes GenSurf really useful, I think. Without his detail hint brushes you'd be much more r_speeds-limited with what you can do with it. I'll be satisfied with this product when I can get similar results in Half-Life - in the current version the r_speeds are just too high, and the older-tech compilers used for Half-Life produce some wonderful visual errors.

Shameless plugs here: You can read more about and/or download MapSpy from http://www.voodooextreme.com/3Fingers/maddog/mapspy/mapspy_index.html,   and GenSurf from http://planetquake.com/gensurf/gensurf.htm.



After spending as much time as you have on the discussion boards, what do you think is the largest problem that beginner level designers struggle with?

That's an easy one, and you already know the answer: Ignorance of (or just flat-out ignoring) the limitations of the game engine. Huge rooms with a lot of detail; that sort of thing. Anyone who's played the only map I've released knows I suffered from this disease myself, so I certainly can't point fingers at anyone. You can preach all you want to beginners about starting small, but I've become convinced it's a waste of time to try to lead new guys in that direction. Some things just have to be learned the hard way.


What games are you really looking forward to in the future?

Quake2 :-) I haven't downloaded the KingPin demo yet, but from everything I've heard it sounds fantastic. I'd like to build a few Half-Life levels - I really enjoy Half-Life's single-player, but since I'm prone to creating polygon-heavy maps I'm afraid that would be a very frustrating experience for me without func_areaportals or hint brushes.

What? No Q3? Heh. I really feel like an outsider sometimes reading posts on the discussion board. I just don't get deathmatch... after 10 minutes or so I'm wondering why I'm not reading a book instead.



How long have you been editing levels?

Not long at all - I'm an old new guy. Quake2 was the first PC game I ever played. I got hooked pretty quickly... well, OK, immediately.


Do you have plans to release any more of your own levels? Maybe a level for a different game?

I don't have any plans as far as level-editing goes. I'm pretty sure I'll try another map sooner or later, but for now I'm pretty well tied up with MapSpy and GenSurf. Mad Dog and I are sitting on go for a Half-Life version of MapSpy - I anticipate a fairly quick release as soon as the game source comes out. Of course as soon as we're satisfied with the HL version we'll have to do the same for KingPin - after that, who knows?


What are your thoughts on the future of the user made level editing community?

I see a big split coming soon unless someone rushes a Q3 SP mod out the door pretty quickly. I've never understood id's decision here, but hey, it's their company. It looks to me like regardless of which path you take level editing is going to continue to get harder - especially single-player maps with all of the new whizbang features being added with each new game.


Seen any good movies lately?

Not really... "Saving Private Ryan" was the most powerful movie I ever recall seeing, but I'm not sure that qualifies as "lately".


Thanks to David for taking the time to answer these questions.
