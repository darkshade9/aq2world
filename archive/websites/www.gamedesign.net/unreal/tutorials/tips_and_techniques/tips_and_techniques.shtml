Tips and Techniques

by Gregor


Example Map

Zones
    Unreal uses a concept of "Zones", which are similar to Areaportals in Quake2, in that they help split up the level for the engine to render it faster. However, they also determine when you are underwater (waterzones), in lava (lavazones) and so forth. Finally, for our purposes, they let you do volumetric fog. Please see epic's tech info webpage for details on using zones. I've found that it's best to extend the edges of the zoneportal polygon past the actual wall of the area, just to be sure.
Selecting
    A handy tool for selecting: I created the staircase in this map, then decided to change the textures on the steps themselves. To do this easily, select one stair, then right-click. Choose the "select" menu, then "select matching items". all of the risers are now selected, and you can change them all simultaneously. There are other handy options in here, so play around.
Texture alignment
    The most handy texture alignment options I saw were "Align as floor/ceiling", "Align wall panning", and "Align wall direction". Experiment to get a feel for these.
Fog
    In order to use fog, you need to customize the properties of the appropriate zone. (Your entire level is automatically considered a single zone if you didn't bother to split it up with zoneportals.) To do this you add a "zoneinfo" actor to your zone. In UnrealEd, use the "Browse" listbox at the top right of the screen and select "Classes". Somewhere in this list you will find "Info". If you expand info, you'll see "ZoneInfo". Select it, then go back to your map editing windows. (If you want a particular type of zone, there are several options under "ZoneInfo".) Right-click where you want the zoneinfo actor, and one of the menu items should say "Add ZoneInfo" here. You should only place one of these per zone, and it doesn't matter where they go as long as the zone is sealed and the actor is somewhere completely inside it.

    Once the ZoneInfo actor is in the zone, select it and right-click to open it's properties. Near the bottom of the properties will be "ZoneInfo". Expand this. In this list will be "FogZone", which is by default set to "false" Change it to "true". Now you can use fog, which is determined by light properties.

    Place a light somewhere within the zone. Open its properties. In the "Lighting" menu you will see the items "VolumeBrightness", "VolumeFog", and "VolumeRadius".

    These three settings determine the fog's characteristics. "VolumeBrightness" determines how well lit the fog is by the light source. "VolumeRadius" determines the apparent distance from you to the fog, and for really small values it seems to be the distance the cloud extends from the light source. I'm not entirely sure what "VolumeFog" does, but I'm sure it must be important. :-)

    Caution: If you place the fog lights too close to a zoneportal, your fog will be abruptly truncated at the portal and will also disappear entirely (even in the areas that just had it) as you cross it. There are also problems with two fog zones that are separated by a non-fog zone and visible from one another. While in either fog zone, you will see the fog in the other, and while in the non-fog zone, you will see no fog in ANY zone. So it's best to keep fog sources around corners or whatever from the zoneportals. None of this applies if the entire level is a single zone.
Playerstarts
    Since it took me awhile to find these, I'll mention it here: Playerstarts are found in "Classes" under "NavigationPoint"
Music
    Load an appropriate .umx file into UnrealEd's browser. Hit F6, or go to the Options menu to open the Level Properties. Under "Audio", choose "song", then select "use". This should place the song name into the "song" area. Due to the mod format, also known as Tracker format, these songs may have several sections to choose from (i.e., for those places in a level where the music changes. I just left it at the default. You may also set the Author name, title, start text, CD tracks to play if the user has CD music instead of digital music enabled, and various default properties of the level. If you recall what I said about the entire level being considered a single zone unless you break it up, this is also the location to set the default zone properties for the level (i.e., anywhere that isn't altered by a ZoneInfo actor). Incidentally, the Music browser is also a handy way to listen to unreal's music while you work, for inspiration.
Error Checking
    You can see if there are any problems with your level (missing title, etc.) by choosing "Validate level" from the options menu. I'm not completely sure what this checks, but I know it at least does that one.
