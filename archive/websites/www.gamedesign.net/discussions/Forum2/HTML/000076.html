Over the last few years I have been closely tied in with the development with Worldcraft. I have seen Quake map design go from a few people beta testing a few level designers to masses of people wanting to get involved! They see maps on servers and say "Hey! I can do that!"

With the advent of editors like Worldcraft it is very feasible for someone to sit down and start building maps. However there are many unwritten rules of level design. I remember the first 2 or 3 maps that I tried to create had to be scrapped due to excessive r-speeds. I took this as a learning step in the advance of my level design skills and knowledge and went onto the next map.

The map makers that have been hired by the companies hopefully have the main attribute required by map makers. Patience! I have compiled and recompiled maps over and over again trying to shave off 50 polygons in a troublesome area.

I honestly have not seen the levels in question. However I do have no plans to download any "public" maps. I have seen far to many first maps which are valiant efforts but really and truly should of been scrapped and left on the map makers hard drive. Maybe one out of a hundred maps released are high quality professional maps.

If your map has excessive rspeeds, it is a failure

If your map will not run in Software, it is a failure

If your map has visable errors, it is a failure.

The true sign of a professional map designer is someone that is willing to bash and fight they map till it gets within the engine limitations. There is a reason why the guys who do this for a living make the big bucks they do. IT IS HARD. On top of that, the other sign is when you know that your map is beyond hope, and its scrapped and you move into another concept.

Now lastly, if you have no knowledge of maps and map making, what gives you the gumption to crtique and review maps when you have no idea what you are talking about. A pretty map allows good looking screen shots to get hits to your webpage. A good map will stay on peoples harddrives and give them a great deal of challenge.

I urge all the young or new mappers out there to rise to the challenge and make a good map. Do familiarize yourself with the maps that game with the game in question. In general these maps will push the limitations of the engine to it limits. If you find yourself making something larger or more complex you are treading on thin ice!

Good luck and good speed marine!

But one other thing I've also been doing is disassembling the various tutorial maps available. Each map I've compiled loaded and tested on my system - not only with an eye towards how they work, but also, to how they don't. It's been quite interesting to go through maps which are slow or experience texture drop out on my system to determine why it is happening. (Side comment - It sure would be nice if more map makers would include their RMF and/or map files when distibuting their BSPs. Good map design not only include looks and layout, but also playability on any system configuration. It seems to be this last factor that many map makers are overlooking in their quest to product that next GEE WHIZ! map.

Just remeber - if the map runs well on a low end system its REALLY going to play well on the high end ones.

So far, of all the third party multiplayer maps I've tried for Half-Life, Tigger-On's have consistantly been the best. They may not be the fanciest or have the most bells and whistles but they do have those key factors that make a great map - good texture use, well thought out layout and weapon placement, and most importantly they run at a good speed even on a low end system.

While I'm not saying that there isn't room for improvement I do feel that Tigger-On's maps would be good basic examples for many map designers to look at when designing their next map.

I've been mapping for a long time and I've seen system requirements slowly going up.

Half-Life has the required system of a P133, 24MB RAM, SVGA, high-color (16-bit), 2X CD-ROM drive, 400MB HD space, Win-compatible soundcard, a mouse, a keyboard, win95/98/NT.

Now, why do you think Valve put that on the box? Just for fun? Do you see 3d Card Required? Hell no! Those requirements are on the box because they are the machine that Valve built Half-Life to run on! Valve took the time to tweak their maps until they ran smoothly in every video mode the game supports and that's what mappers should be doing too!

Sure there are people that have dual pentium systems with an insane ammount of ram and 2 video cards, but the majority of gamers still have low end machines with no video card. I've got a P166 w/24 RAM and no 3d card, Half-Life runs great on it.

I'm forced to play and test my maps in software mode, so naturally I fix any problems that creep up with sky showing up when it's not supposed to or brushes popping in/out of existence. I don't care if the r_speeds are fine, you can still have brushes and sky doing that! It's something that the engine can't handle properly and if the engine can't handle it, it shouldn't be there in the first place.

Anyone can build a map, but it takes someone who is willing to put some time into it and do it right to build a MAP. Mapping is not easy and it's getting more and more complicated. A lot of mappers think that if they're poly counts are low in the .map file that the map is going to run great. Hello? Who told them that? Do you know what compiling a map does to it? The map gets chopped up into smaller chunks, sometimes too many for the engine to properly handle and you get those errors like brushes popping in/out of the map.

All of these things can be done away with very easily. Look at how detail some of the maps in Half-Life are, study them. Yes they are detailed, but that detail comes from good use of textures and great lighting. You'll probably also notice a lot of twists and turns too, those really help to keep the r_speeds down and block the engine from drawing things that it doesn't need to. This is what we had to do with Quake and you damn sure have to do it with Half-Life.

Another thing to keep in mind is not to build large open areas, you're asking for trouble if you do. You need to put things in the map that will block off some areas of that large area like a wall of rocks or a tall building in the middle of it. Look at how Valve did their outdoor areas, study them.

The Half-Life levels and the .map files for some of them that are in the mini sdk can prove quite useful. With the .map file you can actually see what Valve did to keep the map playable!

Something you should also check out, is in Rust's Quake2 Tutorials section, called Polygon Count Reduction Methods. Why do you think those guys put that tutorial together? They did it because they want to show people how to make a great map with a lot of detail and still keep it playable! I suggest that you go and read that tutorial right now, it's excellent.

I guess what I'm really trying to say is that if you're going to build a map, take the time to make sure it runs in all video modes. If you want to build a map to utilize your high end machine, go ahead and do it, but at least say in the text file you need a high end machine to run it. I really believe that maps that have high r_speeds should be be rated normally and then lose 30 or 40 points for not meeting the damn system requirements. It's really easy to build a map with an insane ammount of eye candy, it's hard to build a map with a little eye candy and still make it awesome.

Like one of the tip of the days on my own site say "Don't go overboard with detail! A little eye candy will go a long way."

Being one of the reviewers for Radium, I decided to pop on here and toss in my $.0178 (after taxes). First off, I just have to make the disclaimer that whatever I say here is my opinion and doesn't necessarily reflect the views of Radium or any of the other staff. Not that I'm intending to slam anyone, mind you. Just thought I should make that slight caveat there.

Anyway, here are my own views on the subject. First off, as to our responsability as reviewers. It's always been my belief that since radium has multiple reviewers, and hence multiple systems that the maps are being tested on, it is up to the individual reviewer to describe, at least in brief, the type of system that they used to test the map. And I believe that most people are pretty good at doing that. At least this gives readers some idea of where we're coming from, and if they see a high score for a map but it was tested on a more powerful system, they should at least be aware that (as the saying goes) "individual results may vary."

Having said that, however, I do believe that it's a good idea for us to start playing maps on software mode at least once, so that we can at least comment on obvious flaws. But I also believe that it shouldn't affect the score of reviews so much as the content of the review. For instance, if a map has flaws in software mode but not under hardware, a comment to that effect would be appropriate, but not necessarily a big drop in scores. The reason being is that the map is still a good map, it just suffers from problems inherent to the engine and the mapmaking software. X-fire is a great map, and I love playing it, even on software mode. Certainly I sometimes think "man, if only it didn't have these problems", but it doesn't lower my enjoyment of the map by much.

That leads me (sort of) to the responsability of the mapmakers. We are seeing an increase in the quality of maps coming out for halflife, and that is a good thing. But mapmakers still need to be aware that there are problems with WorldCraft 2.0 (namely missing areaportal), and that the HalfLife engine is designed more with hardware in mind than software, and so things that work great in hardware may not look as good in software.

The next group that needs to take some responsability are the players themselves. People need to start realizing that the trend in gaming is to go to hardware rendering. We all know there will come a day that most games will be hardware only (any that are graphics-intensive, anyway). HalfLife is just one step along the way. While the software renderer is a step above Quake2's, it's two steps below what it could be if it were the primary renderer. I understand that Valve did a great job making its maps run on software as well as hardware, but the truth is, most of their maps aren't earth-shattering. They're just decent maps (considering that they're professionally designed). We talk about raising the bar on the level of map design, and while some of that can be accomplished by skills alone, is it really a bad thing that we use the ability of the hardware renderer as part of the means to increase the level of maps coming out? I don't really think so.

The final area of responsability falls to Valve and the next version of WorldCraft. Now, I don't know exactly how areaportal and such work. I don't know if it would require a total redesign of the engine or not, but if not, I think it'd be a definate boon to mapmakers everywhere if there were speed-enhancing elements such as this added to future versions of WorldCraft.

So, to sum up:
Reviewers - take a bit of time to run in software and at least note possible problems. But realize the good stuff may be hard on lower end systems.

Mapmakers - if you want your level playable by all, keep in mind that big, open, high-poly areas are not conductive to that. If you design a level for hardware only, be prepared to make some people unhappy. But don't put off making the next great map just because it won't work well in software.

Players - I think that most people playing on the internet should know enough that if a map runs great on a PII 400 with a Voodoo II, it doesn't mean it'll run great on their 166 in software. If you have a lower end system, take note of what a reviewer has to say about the kind of system he's using, and caveat emptor (let the downloader beware?)

Valve - Give the mapmapkers something better to work with the next time around. WorldCraft 2 is great, but it could be much better if there were performance enhancements.

Well, I hope that makes sense. If not, remember it's just my opinion.

The topic of "Playable Maps" (the subject of this thread, in case anyone has forgotten) is an important one, and it's obviously one where people have strong opinions, otherwise this thread wouldn't be nearly so long (nor would many of the individual posts).

I see 3 major views represented here:


  • Making maps within the limitation of the game engine

  • Making maps that are fun to play

  • Making maps that are showcases of architectural design

  • Making maps that are all 3 of the above

To which I add another:

  • Making maps that run like Valve's maps do

Why do I add this? For a very simple reason, one which perhaps Radium (who is a professed non-mapper) should appreciate: Someone who runs Half-Life on his machine would tend to expect a Half-Life map he downloads to run the same. What does this person know about r_speeds? Nothing. What does he know about engine limitations? Nothing. But he knows how HL runs, because he played the game. He sees a HL map available, and he has a reasonable expectation that this map will run in a similar fashion.

Putting a tiny link to the text file is no solution, this is similar to telling people to read the fine print. Most will read the review's "Bottom Line", and say, "fine rating, time to click 'download'".

It is the "Bottom Line" rating that is misleading. Look at the points I outlined above, those are the major points of this thread, and should be the major ratings points in the review summary.


  1. If a map stays within the limits of the game engine, and employs entities properly without goofy things happening, it should get a good technical rating. This would include things like enemy/item placement, lighting, detail, and texturing. If polys disappear do to high r_speeds, the map should take a hit here. Sorry SlowHand, but "fuck the r_speeds" is rather absurd.

  2. If a map is fun to play, there you go, a fun-to-play rating. This would include cahllenge, strategy, and also play balance. No one's map need have their ratings downgraded here, if their map is less-than-perfect in another area.

  3. If a map looks like a dream, well you have a high level design rating. Here's where architecture, layout, theme, and ambience comes in. Even if it is unplayable and has technical flaws.

  4. Finally, to cover the point I brought up, minimum system requirements can be defined. This should satisfy those who want to make maps not limited by software rendering, while clearing warning off those who can't run them properly. Respect for those who pay for connect time is maintained.

Running a poll to determine accurately which radio buttons are clicked, by those who vote, out of those who visit Radium's site over the next few days -- only tells us what radio buttons are clicked by those people over this limited time. It does not tell us what systems people are running. And it is not an alternative to honestly rating maps against the standard Valve established. Like it or not, they did establish a standard. Is that standard to be ignored because they're not idsoftware?

Scrap the poll, and revamp the ratings. Maps that are strong in certain areas will be rated as such, and if they have limitations in others so be it. Give people something they can judge for themselves, don't attempt to spoon-feed them 'overall' ratings.

Now Radium, this is not a flame so please don't take it as such. You may feel otherwise but I think I made some halfway decent suggestions here.
