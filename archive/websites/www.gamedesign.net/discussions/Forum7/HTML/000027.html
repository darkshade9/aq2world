Yeah, the new ArghRad looks awesome. I did some research on Phong shading, for anyone who's interested on it's differences from other shading algorithms:
(Acording to Mike Morrison, a computer animation publisher, in his book "Becoming A Computer Animator")

"Phong shading is a 3D surface shading method developed by Phong Bui-Toung in 1974. His shading method accurately interpolates the colors over a polygonal surface, giving accurate reflective highlights and shading. [Quake 2 doesn't really have much in the way of highlights though.] The drawback to this is that Phong shading can be up to 100 times slower than Gouraud shading.
[OK, this was written 1994, and computers were slower back then. Though in Quake all lighting isn't caluculated dynamically, Phong shading today is relatively fast.
Gouraud was invented in 1971 by Henri Gouraud. It hardly takes any more caluculation time than flat, or constant, shading, which made it really impressive. Back then at least. It was better than making smooth objects with lots of faces. When Phong shading came along, Gouraud's method was only then used on non-important items in rendering, such as background items, where speed was more important than accuracy.]
...As with Gouraud shading, Phong shading cannot smoothe over the outer edges of 3D objects.
[This is evident in the example screenshot on the Rust News page. We can't blame Tim for that right? ]"

There is another, more modern shading algorithm called Blinn Shading, invented by James Blinn (who also invented bump mapping BTW; smart guy). It is like Phong shading, but produces more accurate, rounder highlights than Phong shading, without the soften effects needed in Phong shading to get the same effect. Also, when the light glances a surface at a low angle, Blinn shading produces an accurate highlight where Phong shading cannot.

OK, just a little bit of computer graphics history there, for those of you interested in where these weird words came from.
