RaVeN:

ATA/100 has a theoretical limit of 100 MBytes/second, and since there are 8 bits in a byte (unless you're talking TCP/IP but that's another discussion...), that equals 800Mbps. Unfortunately even ATA/100 doesn't come very close to its limit. The best 'Burst' rate I have seen on an ATA/100 is ~67MB/Sec or 536Mbps... And as people who are into hardware, particularly hard drives will know, 'burst' speeds are few and far between in actual practice. Sustained transfer rates are ~37 MB/Sec for a good drive. This means that a good quality 7,200 RPM ATA/100 HDD will give a sustained transfer rate of 296Mbps...
ATA/66 run about the same as ATA/100 since data bandwidth is the bottleneck, but ATA/33 runs a little more than half as fast as ATA/66/100... And remember this is just for SIDE/ATA... SCSI is a whole 'nother game. So is throwing raid 0 into the mix. Both of them will increase your throughput to some extent, and if you have SCSI RAID, then the performance can be even better (though SIDE-RAID ATA/66/100 is closing the performance gap with SCSI... )

As far as cable vs. xDSL, I have cable, and have on occasion maxed my 10Mbps NIC during downloads... And since they adopted the DOCSIS standard last year, I can count the number of times the system has gone down on one hand and have a few left over... Only problem I have is that they state on their page that permanent and semi-permanent web or game servers are a no-no. I think I'll eventually test that out for myself though. I haven't detected them port sniffing, so hopefully I'll be safe awhile.

Also for those who don't know:

When dealing with bits and bytes in any form the proper notation is {b = bits} & {B = Bytes} Therefore when giving data transfer rates, it would be:

1236.4 Kbps
151.5 KB/sec

Also when talking about TCP/IP and other transfer/communication protocol a Byte is not neccessarily 8-bits... and because of this 'overhead' you will not neccessarily get your max. rated speed. Extra bits are required for addressing, etc.
